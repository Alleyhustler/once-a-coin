ONCE A COIN

"All currency bleeds, but only some remember their first sin."

You're holding something that was once a coin. Smooth now from centuries of touch, its markings worn to ghosts. Kings and queens have held it, beggars and thieves have traded it, wars were fought for its likeness. Now it rests in your palm—weightless, faceless, its purpose forgotten.

As you rub your thumb across its surface, the metal grows warm. In the dim light, you see faint etchings reappear—not of nations or rulers, but of a door. A gate. A threshold. The coin thrums with something older than mint marks, older than money.
